II
The Beginning

HOW IT
ALL BEGAN

George Remus — Cincinnati's King of the Bootleggers — found the medicinal alcohol loophole and built a bootlegging empire that made Al Capone look like an amateur. When federal agents caught him, his entire network relocated across the river to Newport, Kentucky, where city government was entirely for sale.

The geography was perfect: the Ohio River created jurisdictional confusion, the city was small enough to bribe entirely, and Cincinnati's booming respectable population wanted somewhere to be disrespectable on Friday night. When Prohibition ended in 1933, the bootleggers simply pivoted. As NKU Professor Dr. Paul Tenkotte explains: “Many of the bootleggers just segue-wayed themselves right into the gambling business.”

Newport was the predecessor to Las Vegas — a manufactured vacation destination where tourists flocked under the cover of isolation and darkness.

— Dr. Paul Tenkotte, NKU History Professor

The mob moved into an area that was small enough that the government and police could be bribed.

— Historian Hackett, Newport Mob Research

IV
The Establishments

THE JOINTS

Newport had two tiers: bust-out joints (rough, rigged, dangerous) and carpet joints — mob-run palaces of luxury rivaling anything in America. $30 million a year, after dark, in Northern Kentucky.

The Beverly Hills Club
Carpet Joint · A-List Showroom
The crown jewel. Liberace, Milton Berle, Steve Lawrence, and Eydie Gormé performed live. Owned by Pete Schmidt, it set the gold standard. The Vegas Rat Pack was effectively being born in Newport, Kentucky.
Glenn Schmidt's Playtorium
Mob HQ · Casino · Bowling
Bowling alley, bar, and casino under one roof — official Syndicate headquarters. Today it's the Syndicate Events Center and Gangsters Dueling Piano Bar. The building still stands in Newport.
The Lookout House
Carpet Joint · Wilder, KY
Placed at the top of a hill — operators could see police coming from miles away. By the time police arrived, all gambling evidence had vanished into hidden rooms. Guests were simply having dinner.
Mustang Betty's
Full Service · Monmouth Street
Three floors of vice: brothel upstairs, drinking and gambling below, and a nationwide phone-betting network in the basement. Now an antique store. Go look at it next time you cross the bridge, Arnie.
The Yorkshire Club
Casino · Race Book
7,500 sq ft casino with racetrack result boards lining the rear wall. Results poured in from tracks nationwide. One of Newport's biggest betting operations, owned by gangsters Joe and Martin Berman.
The Glenn Hotel & Tropicana
Hotel · Nightclub · Crime Scene
Where the mob made its fatal mistake: drugging and framing George Ratterman with stripper April Flowers. So clumsy it brought Bobby Kennedy's FBI. One botched room, one botched night — and the mob lost everything.
V
The Downfall

THE NIGHT
IT ALL ENDED

The fall of Newport's empire is one of the most dramatic stories in American criminal history — a drugged football hero, Bobby Kennedy, a decapitated witness, and a quiet departure that felt like the end of an era.

Shortly before 3 a.m., reform candidate George Ratterman — Notre Dame football legend running for Campbell County Sheriff — was found half-naked in bed at the Glenn Hotel with exotic dancer April Flowers (real name: Juanita Hodges). Ratterman had zero memory of how he got there. The night before, he’d had one drink with mob associate Tito Carinci in Cincinnati. One drink. That was it — he’d been drugged. The setup was so transparently contrived that it immediately backfired.
U.S. Attorney General Robert F. Kennedy was on a crusade against organized crime and looking to make his mark. The Ratterman case was his opportunity. He sent dozens of FBI agents to Newport. The federal government prosecuted the mob for violating Ratterman's civil rights. The trials lasted years. The government won. NKU itself was founded partly because of the federal investment Kennedy's involvement triggered in Campbell County.
Juanita Hodges — April Flowers — turned on the mob and became a star witness. Her testimony was devastating. She was found decapitated in Miami a few years later. The murder was never solved. She had seen too much and known too much. In the world she moved through, that was always fatal.
Ratterman was elected sheriff and served four years. In his New York Times obituary he said: “The other side knew what was coming, and they knew we knew.” Newport — the original Sin City, the city that ran America's vices for three decades — went quiet. The gangsters moved to a desert in Nevada where gambling was actually legal. They built something much bigger.

VI
Original Las Vegas

FREMONT STREET:
WHERE IT STARTED

Las Vegas was born from a railroad deal. In 1902, Senator William Clark bought 1,800 acres to make it a train stop. In 1905, he held a land auction — over 1,000 investors arrived from LA and Salt Lake City. Many never went home. Fremont Street was ground zero for every Las Vegas “first”: first hotel (Hotel Nevada, 1906, now the Golden Gate), first telephone (1907), first paved road (1925), first traffic light and first gaming license (both 1931).

Then 1931 brought two simultaneous miracles: Nevada legalized gambling, AND Hoover Dam construction began. The dam brought 5,000 workers to the desert. Gambling brought their paychecks back to Fremont Street. The city that had been 800 people in 1909 exploded into a national phenomenon.

VII
Birth of the Strip

THE STRIP

The Strip — that stretch of Las Vegas Boulevard the whole world pictures when they think “Las Vegas” — didn't exist until 1941. Thomas Hull opened El Rancho Vegas on Highway 91 south of the city, mostly as a motel for tired drivers. Its success immediately attracted the most dangerous and ambitious businessmen in America.

Las Vegas was particularly valuable because Nevada was the only state permitting off-track betting. By the mid-1940s, Bugsy Siegel was receiving $25,000 per month in race wire payments from Las Vegas alone. But he wanted bigger. He found Billy Wilkerson — founder of the Hollywood Reporter — who had started building a luxury casino hotel and run out of money. Siegel forced his way in with mob cash.

The Flamingo was named after Siegel's girlfriend Virginia Hill — nicknamed “The Flamingo” for her red hair and long legs. It opened December 26, 1946 with Siegel in a tuxedo and pink carnation, Jimmy Durante performing, and George Raft in the audience. It was a financial disaster. Then Siegel was murdered. The mob took over. By 1948, it turned $4 million profit. Every crime family in America now wanted a piece of Las Vegas.

The Flamingo's sleek design and luxury amenities represented a dramatic turn for a town that had embraced a dust-covered Old West theme. Siegel ultimately vindicated himself, even in death.

VIII
The Golden Age

THE MOB'S
LAS VEGAS

After Siegel's murder, experienced mob businessmen ran Las Vegas like a corporation — except for the skimming. The same men from Newport, Cleveland, and New York built the golden era of the Strip.

1941

The Strip Is Born

Thomas Hull opens El Rancho Vegas on Highway 91 — the first resort on what will become the Las Vegas Strip. The casino is almost an afterthought. Its success changes everything.

1946

The Flamingo Opens

Bugsy Siegel opens the most lavish casino in American history on December 26. Jimmy Durante performs. It loses $300,000 in the first week. Siegel is shot dead six months later. The Flamingo makes $4M in 1948.

1950

Moe Dalitz Arrives

The same Moe Dalitz who ran Northern Kentucky's criminal empire with the Cleveland Four opens the Desert Inn. Newport's organizational genius has officially arrived in the Nevada desert.

1952

The Sands Opens

With mob financing, the Sands becomes the home of the Rat Pack — Sinatra, Dean Martin, Sammy Davis Jr. The same celebrities who gambled in Newport's back rooms now headline legally in Nevada.

1961

Newport Falls. Vegas Rises.

Bobby Kennedy shuts down Newport. The mob, stripped of their Kentucky base, is now fully committed to Nevada. The Strip becomes the undisputed center of American entertainment.

1966

Caesars Palace Opens

The Strip reaches its peak mob-era glory. Caesars Palace — Italian Renaissance fantasy in the Nevada desert — sets the template for mega-resort Las Vegas. Howard Hughes begins buying Strip properties.

1989

Steve Wynn & The Mirage

The corporate era begins. The Mirage costs $630 million and opens as the first true megaresort. The mob era is definitively over. The billion-dollar resort era has begun.

1995

Fremont Street Experience

$70 million revitalizes downtown with a 1,375-foot LED canopy. Old Vegas gets its renaissance. 26 million visitors a year. Vegas Vic still waves from the Pioneer Club, watching it all.

The Final Word

NEWPORT was the proving ground LAS VEGAS was the graduation ceremony

The same men who bribed Newport's police, built the Beverly Hills Club, ran the numbers rackets, and attracted Frank Sinatra to a Kentucky backroom — those same men packed their money and their methods and moved to a desert where the law worked in their favor.

They didn't escape justice. They went legitimate.
And in doing so, they built the most visited entertainment destination in the history of the world.
